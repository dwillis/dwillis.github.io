Money and Leadership in the Presbyterian Church in America

One more deep dive into a very valuable dataset

A couple of years ago, I was at city hall in my little town when I got caught in a conversation with our assistant city manager. I mentioned that I was a professor at EIU at the time, and that we had a lot of students studying public administration and public policy. In fact, many of our recent graduates wanted to do exactly what he was doing for a living.

He said something that’s really stuck with me — and I think it highlights one of academia’s biggest problems. The kinds of questions we try to answer in the ivory tower just don’t line up with the ones people in the field actually need answered.

For example, he wanted to know: How much money should a city keep in reserves to supplement its general fund during an economic downturn? What a practical and important question. Yet, despite earning a concentration in public administration in grad school, I’d never seen a single article about that topic.

That same disconnect exists in academic research on religion, too. There’s a huge gap between what pastors and denominational leaders want to know and what academics are interested in studying.

Today, I want to try—at least in my own small way—to build a rickety bridge across that divide. I’m going to dig into two things every church cares about: money and leadership.

Using the detailed data provided by the Presbyterian Church in America (PCA), I’ll give you a general sense of what a “normal” church looks like for its size along a few key dimensions.

For instance: how many people should be in formal leadership roles?

In the PCA, there are two offices—deacons and elders. Elders handle teaching and governance. A pastor is an elder, for instance, but there are also ruling elders who serve on the Session (the church’s governing body) without ever stepping behind a pulpit.

Deacons, on the other hand, focus on “mercy ministry” and maintaining the property—pretty similar to how the term is used in many Baptist churches.

So, how many deacons and elders does the average PCA church have? To answer that, we need to break things down by church size.

In the very smallest churches, you’ll usually find about three people serving in formal leadership roles. That number rises to around four deacons and elders in churches with 26–50 members. As congregations grow, leadership naturally expands with them. A church with 100–250 members averages about ten leaders, and among the largest PCA churches, that number climbs to roughly 37.

What really stands out to me is how consistent the ratio between deacons and elders stays, no matter the size of the church. For every one deacon, there’s one elder. It’s a near-perfect 50/50 split that barely wobbles across the entire dataset.

Then I wanted to answer a simple, practical question: How many new members does a church typically need before it adds someone to formal leadership?

Across the entire sample, the average church has one leader for every twenty members. That’s the quick “one-number” answer—but honestly, I’m not sure how useful it is.

In the last church I pastored, for example, attendance went from about 50 people in 2006 down to a dozen by 2024. Near the end, we used to joke that half our members were on the church council—and that wasn’t much of an exaggeration. These ratios can vary wildly depending on the size and season of a congregation.

In a really small church, there’s often one elder or deacon for every seven members—which is, frankly, pretty inefficient. But if your congregation only has twenty people, you still need more than one leader, right? As churches grow, though, that ratio begins to stretch. Among churches with 50–100 members, there’s roughly one leader for every sixteen people—double the ratio of the smallest congregations.

Then, compare that group to churches with at least 500 members, and the ratio doubles again. In the largest PCA churches, there’s about one leader for every thirty-three members.

That’s why it’s nearly impossible to offer a single, one-size-fits-all answer to the question of leadership staffing. Still, as a general rule of thumb, one new leader for every 20–25 new members seems like a pretty reasonable benchmark.

Now, let’s talk about money.

I’m not even sure how pastors or denominational leaders think about this one. But I imagine a lot of them wrestle with it during budget season: Does our church’s income look normal for our size? What’s the average amount given per member?

These aren’t the kinds of questions that usually get discussed in denominational meetings—at least not in my experience.

Fortunately, the PCA data includes a helpful metric called “giving per capita.” It’s simply the total amount of tithes and offerings divided by the total number of members.

Across the entire PCA dataset—more than 1,700 churches—about 7% have a per-capita giving rate below $1,000 per person. If that’s where your church lands, it’s well outside the norm. But so is the opposite extreme: per-capita giving above $7,000, which describes only about 5% of PCA congregations.

(My guess? Those churches probably have one or two exceptionally generous donors who skew the numbers at the top end.)

Most churches fall somewhere in the middle. The most common range is $2,000 to $4,000 per member, which includes nearly half of all congregations in the dataset. So if you’re trying to get a sense of what “normal” looks like, that’s the sweet spot.

The median per-capita giving amount is $3,314. If you’re looking for a single benchmark, that’s about as close to an exact figure as we can get.

But of course, the size of the congregation matters here too.

I was actually surprised by how little church size seemed to matter when I calculated the mean per-capita giving across six size categories.

Yes, the number is a bit higher among the very smallest churches—and that makes sense. They still have the same basic infrastructure costs as everyone else: maintenance, utilities, insurance, and so on. Our building didn’t shrink as our congregation did, and that’s something we wrestled with near the end. Fewer people were carrying the same financial load.

But when you compare churches with memberships between 26 and 500, the differences are surprisingly modest. Per-capita giving is about $3,500 per person in smaller congregations and rises only to around $3,850 among those at the upper end—a bump of roughly 10%.

You do see a noticeable jump among the very largest churches, though: about $4,343 per person in congregations with 500 or more members.

I wouldn’t have guessed that at all. I actually expected the opposite. It seems easier to “free ride” in a big church—people might assume others will pick up the slack. But that’s not what the data shows. Instead, the largest churches in the PCA are also home to some of the most generous members.

Now let’s move from income to the other side of the ledger: expenses.

The PCA data includes a column for “current expenses,” which falls under the heading of congregational operational disbursements. To get a sense of how churches manage their budgets, I divided this figure by total income.

Across the average PCA church, about 86% of all income goes out through current expenses. In other words, the typical church saves roughly 14% of its income each year.

But, once again, church size plays a big role in how that balance shakes out.

It probably won’t shock anyone to learn that small churches are spending more than they’re taking in. In fact, their expenses exceed their income by nearly 30%.

I know exactly what that feels like. In my last church, we had to “borrow” from our wills and bequests fund every year just to keep the lights on. That’s what I’d call the death spiral of small congregations—once membership drops below a certain threshold, it becomes almost impossible to recover, for both social and financial reasons.

The picture looks a lot better for churches that are even slightly larger. Congregations with 26 to 250 members spend between 85% and 88% of their income each year, which means they’re still managing to save a decent amount. And for the very largest churches, the numbers look even stronger.

Among churches with at least 500 members, every three dollars that comes in translates to just two dollars going out for expenses. That’s a savings rate north of 30%. It’s a great example of economies of scale in action. Doubling from 300 to 600 members doesn’t double your costs—but it often does double your giving. That’s how you build financial breathing room.

There was one more column in the spreadsheet that caught my eye: building fund offerings.

I’m guessing most churches have something like this—a dedicated pot of money for expanding the sanctuary or renovating existing spaces. What surprised me was how common it was: exactly one-third of all PCA churches had a line item for building funds.

And that got me thinking: wouldn’t you expect churches that are growing to be more likely to have a building fund than those that are shrinking?

Well, yeah—but not by much. In this dataset, about 34% of churches that reported more subtractions than additions in the prior year still contributed to a building fund. Among those that grew in the past year, the figure was 41%. And for churches that didn’t change at all, it dropped to just 9%.

There’s probably an easy explanation for why so many shrinking churches still have a building fund. Maybe the previous year was just a blip—they’d been growing for a while, but something happened that temporarily slowed them down. Then again, maybe not.

What’s just as interesting is that plenty of growing churches don’t have money set aside for physical expansion. In the end, it really does seem to come down to each congregation’s philosophy about planning and stewardship.

To wrap this all up, I recreated a scatter plot I’ve used before. On the x-axis is each congregation’s total membership, and on the y-axis is total church income.

Here’s a formula that I think will be genuinely useful for anyone trying to estimate how much income their church should have based on total membership:

Total Church Income = (3,519.40 × Total Members)-42,716

So, for example, a church with 100 members would have an expected income of about $309,000.

That’s a question I get all the time: How many members do we need before we’re “self-sufficient”?

Well, this is my best estimate based on the PCA data. Is the PCA perfectly representative on this metric? Honestly, I have no idea. If I had similar data from even two or three other denominations, I could build a much more confident model.

(And if you’re a denominational leader who has access to this kind of data, I’d love to see it. You don’t need to share anything identifying—just two columns: membership and income for each church. That’s all I need to run the analysis.)

But for now, this is the best snapshot I can offer with the data that’s available.

If nothing else, I hope it helps pastors and church leaders get a clearer sense of what “normal” looks like.
