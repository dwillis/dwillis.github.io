When Are Half Your Members Going to be Dead?

The tipping point for many denominations is not that far away.

Jan 29, 2026

I’m a bit enamored recently with the life of Ernest Hemingway. He was obviously a tremendous writer, maybe one of the finest in our country’s history. But he also lived a life that could charitably be described as “chaotic.” He moved constantly, was married four times, struggled with alcoholism his entire life, and suffered at least a dozen serious concussions from plane crashes and car accidents.

As you can probably guess, his financial situation was equally tumultuous. He was paid the modern-day equivalent of $55,000 for writing a 3,000-word piece for Collier’s magazine in 1944. He also sold tens of thousands of copies of his books during his lifetime. But he was always teetering on the verge of insolvency. In The Sun Also Rises, one of the main characters, Mike Campbell, seemed to echo the author’s own sentiments when asked, “How did you go bankrupt?” Mike responded, “Two ways — gradually, then suddenly.”

That’s a line I think about a whole lot when I’m talking to churches about the future of their local congregation and their larger denominational tradition. I cannot emphasize this point enough — we are in a lull right now. While most major denominations have been experiencing decline for a while, their ship has remained seaworthy. Yeah, some water will lap over the sides every once in a while, but there are still enough buckets and enough laborers to toss it back into the ocean.

That won’t be the case in a very short time horizon, and I don’t think many people realize just how quickly the buckets and the workers are going to disappear.

Let me start making that point with a graph you’ve seen before — just a simple age distribution of the 20 largest denominations in the Cooperative Election Study. (These aren’t the 20 largest denominations in the United States; they’re just the groups respondents choose most often.)

Here’s one point I need to make here (and will reiterate later): this is only adults. There are no large-scale surveys of children in Protestant denominations. I wouldn’t even know how to begin thinking about undertaking something like that. Just pondering how to get it through my university’s Institutional Review Board makes me shudder. But this data does give us a good sense of what the 18-and-older population looks like right now.

This should come as a shock to no one who is vaguely aware of American religion — Episcopalians are old. In fact, two-thirds of their adult members have celebrated their 60th birthday. In contrast, just 6% are under the age of thirty. Put simply: for every young adult Episcopalian in the pews this Sunday, there will be about ten retirees. Oof.

And they aren’t alone in this. The Lutheran Church–Missouri Synod is in almost exactly the same spot. So are the United Methodists, the Presbyterian Church (USA), and the Evangelical Lutherans. You basically see a whole bunch of mainline Protestants clustered at the top of this graph.

But let me also point out that some big evangelical groups aren’t exactly swimming in young adults, either. For instance, just 6% of Southern Baptist adults are under the age of 30. That’s no different at all than the Episcopalians. Only about one in five are under the age of 45. The Assemblies of God is doing a bit better here, but their numbers are still pretty anemic — 11% in the 18–29 bucket and 19% in the 30–44 age group.

Let me now configure the data into a pretty straightforward metric: the share of each of these denominations who belong to the Baby Boomer generation (born 1946–1964).

I hate to say this, but I am always considering what kind of graph will go viral on social media, and this is certainly the one with the highest likelihood. In short, Baby Boomers dominate most large Protestant denominations. I mean, half of United Methodists and Episcopalians were born during that 18-year window. And for 16 of the 20 denominations on this list, Boomers make up at least 40% of their adult membership.

In the population of all adults in the United States, what share are Baby Boomers? It’s about 20%. Just ruminate on that for a minute: Boomers are twice as prominent among Methodists, Baptists, and Episcopalians as they are in the general population. This is the point I want to make very clearly: the only reason American Protestant Christianity is not in absolute free fall right now is because it’s being buoyed by the Baby Boomers.

For what it’s worth, I think you all should read Derek Thompson’s missive about how odd the Boomers really are. Between 1935 and 1958, the fertility rate in the United States rose by 50%. Then it dropped in half by the early 1970s. I can say this with absolute certainty — we will never see something like that again in the United States. Not even close.

Let me give you another statistical angle on this data. In the graph above, I’m showing two descriptive statistics. One is the mean (or average). That’s the easy one: add up all the ages and divide by the number of respondents.

The other is the mode. That’s the age that appears most often in the dataset. Think of it this way: imagine all the Southern Baptists in the survey are listed in a spreadsheet, and you have a random number generator pick one row. The age you’re most likely to draw is the mode.

Including both the mean and the mode helps reveal not just the typical age but also where the largest share of people actually cluster—giving a more complete picture of the age distribution.

The means are denoted in red, and you can see that most of the numbers are at least 55 years old. For instance, the average adult member of an Episcopal church is 61 years old. For Southern Baptists, it’s 58. The Assemblies of God are doing a bit better on this metric at 54.

But look at the modal age for a lot of these groups—this is probably the most important metric when it comes to understanding the future of American Protestant Christianity. For many, the modal age is significantly higher than the mean. In a bunch of mainline Protestant denominations, it’s between 67 and 69 years old. For the Assemblies of God, the modal age is 69, which is 15 years older than the mean.

What does this mean? Well, the mean can be pulled down by a decent chunk of younger folks. If you have a good core of 30-year-olds, they can counterbalance those 60-year-olds in that calculation. That’s not true for the mode. It gives you a much better sense of when “the tipping point” is going to come.

A beeswarm plot is the most effective way to visualize this phenomenon. Where the swarm is fatter, that represents more people in the distribution. So you can really quickly point out the “bulges” in the population pyramid. I’ve also marked the modal age with a black line across each distribution.

In the top row, compare the Assemblies of God (in the dark blue) to the United Methodists (in the bright red). Notice how the AG has a higher modal age than the UMC (69 vs. 67), yet the mean age for the AG is about seven years younger (54 vs. 61). When you look at the beeswarm distribution, you get a much more complete picture of what’s going to happen to each denomination in the near future.

The AG has a pretty noticeable bulge at the bottom of their plot — lots of people in their thirties and forties. The United Methodists, by contrast, are much “skinnier” in that same area. They simply don’t have large numbers of younger adults. Yes, the AG’s modal age is slightly higher than the UMC’s, but look at the spread just above and below the black line for each denomination. For the UMC, that band is still very wide on both sides of the mode. That’s not the case with the AG.

What this means in practical terms is that the AG will lose numbers fairly quickly when its modal age hits the end of life, but those losses won’t keep compounding afterward. For the United Methodists, though, there’s a long tail of older members who will continue aging out for years after the modal age passes.

This is why I’m much more optimistic about the future of the Church of Christ than I am about the Southern Baptists. Look how skinny the SBC distribution is at the bottom of the plot. When that huge bulge begins to age into their eighties, the membership of the Convention will begin to decline incredibly rapidly. There are just not nearly enough young people to offset those losses. In the Churches of Christ, meanwhile, there’s a whole cluster of twentysomethings.

Those young people are so incredibly important to the future of any denomination. This is what I call “The Iron Law of Church Growth.” If you have a bunch of gray-haired folks and not many kids, it’s almost impossible to pull out of that death spiral. I calculated the percentage of each tradition that is between the ages of 18 and 40 — that’s “peak fertility” ages. These are the people most likely to bring new life into the church through the nursery.

You can see that these young people used to be a lot more plentiful in these denominations fifteen years ago than they are today. For the Evangelical Lutherans, 28% were 18–40 years old around 2010. Today, that share is down to just 21%. For other mainline groups like the Episcopalians and the United Methodists, these young adults have also dropped by four and five percentage points, respectively.

But evangelicals haven’t managed to dodge this bullet, either. In 2008–2010, 28% of Southern Baptists were in their peak fertility years. In the most recent data, that’s down to just 19%. That’s right in line with most mainline churches. And there have been big drops among non-denominational groups, too. Among non-denom evangelicals, the young adult share has fallen from 39% to 27%.

A church without a full nursery is one that is careening toward demographic disaster.

But I wanted to pull together one final bit of analysis that will hopefully make this reality even more “real” to everyone. Here’s what I did: I assumed that folks would live to be 85 years old. I know plenty of people live beyond that, but how many of them are still vibrant, active, engaged members of a church in their late 80s or early 90s? The answer is not that many.

Using that assumption, I estimated the year in which at least 30% of adult members would no longer be active, and then did the same for the 40% and 50% benchmarks. That’s what appears below.

The denominations at the top have the longest time horizon. For instance, the Free Will Baptists won’t lose 30% of their adult membership until 2048. For the Assemblies of God, that number is just slightly earlier: 2044. I mean, that’s two decades away, right? And it’s only 30% of the adult membership. That’s not too catastrophic, and those shifts will be mitigated somewhat by children moving into adult membership in those churches.

But look at the denominations at the bottom. These are groups that should be in “code red” mode right now and should be planning for a future that looks much different from the past. Take the United Methodists, for instance. Their membership will drop by 30% in the next fifteen years. Okay, not great, but that’s just two points per year, and they have some time to plan for that. But here’s the rest of the story: they will lose 30% in 15 years, then another 10% four years later, then another 10% three years after that. Their membership will be cut in half in just about 22 years.

This is exactly what I mean when I say that there’s a tipping point in this membership data. Decline is not going to follow some gradual linear process. That’s not what’s going to happen. Decline will happen very gradually — then all at once. There are a ton of denominations on this list, really prominent ones, that will lose 20% of their membership in the blink of an eye.

Now, I know what some of you are thinking: this doesn’t consider how many kids will replace the folks lost through death. You’re right — I can’t calculate that for each denomination because I don’t have enough data. But I do have a little glimpse from a couple of groups that publish those statistics. In the Episcopal Church, 13% of members are under the age of 18. In the Presbyterian Church (USA), it’s just 4%.

I am more than willing to admit that the numbers in the graph above are the worst-case scenario. They don’t consider inflows at all. But does anyone really believe that bringing children into the equation alters the timeline in a significant way? It may push back that 50% threshold by five to ten years for some denominations. But now the decline is in 2055 instead of 2048.

I know this is going to sound harsh, but when I present this kind of information, lots of folks convey a sense of concern — yet at the end of the day they think to themselves, “I’m 62 years old. This won’t be my problem to solve.” But there are others who want to steer their denomination in a way that actually makes sense in light of this future.

Here’s my advice: a planned and deliberate series of small procedures is far superior to an emergency amputation. In other words, cutting the budget 2–3% per year every single year for the next twenty years is far superior to making a 35% cut in a single year in 2045. If someone retires, just don’t replace them. That’s much less painful than firing someone. If a program’s director leaves your denomination, it may be time to scale down that program.

Making these types of strategic decisions now is the mark of true leadership. It’s easy to kick the can down the road and leave the mess for someone else. That’s the mark of a coward.

Do the hard thing. Plan for the inevitable. The tipping point is coming.

Ryan P. Burge is a professor of practice at the Danforth Center on Religion and Politics at Washington University.
