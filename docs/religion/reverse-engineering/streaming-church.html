The People Streaming Church Aren’t Who You Think

Education, income, and the hidden class pattern in religious attendance

Ryan Burge
Feb 12, 2026
∙ Paid
Article voiceover

I’ve been thinking a whole lot about social isolation recently. It’s probably because it’s this unspoken concept in a lot of the work that I do and many of the questions that I’m asked about religion in the United States. I swear I bring up Robert Putnam’s Bowling Alone about twice a week when I’m doing interviews or giving presentations about data on religious attendance. Putnam saw an America that was rapidly hurtling toward social atomization. He charted the decline in membership in social clubs and bowling leagues.

You know what he blamed it on? Cable television. And there’s a very good reason for that—he was writing his masterpiece in the mid-1990s, when that was the major entertainment innovation. Isn’t that quaint? Now we don’t have 100 channels; we have a million channels, plus every streaming service imaginable. It’s easier to distract ourselves now than ever before.

I see church attendance as a keyhole into how people manage to socialize today. It’s one of the last voluntary social activities that Americans still do in large numbers. So the choice of whether to attend, with what frequency, and in what modality (either in person or online) is an ideal way to try to assess how the average person is thinking about “touching grass” right now.

To that end, I want to explore two factors that I think about all the time when it comes to the determinants of socialization: educational attainment and household income. I’m using the Pew Religious Landscape Study, which is exceptionally helpful here because it asks about attending religious services both in person and online.

Let me start by just showing you how education interacts with in-person religious attendance across a range of birth cohorts.

The top-left graph is the entire sample thrown together—nearly 37,000 respondents. One thing you can see here is something that I write about all the time: educated people are more likely to attend a house of worship this weekend than those who have only obtained a high school diploma. The difference from the bottom of the scale to the top is three percentage points (25% → 28%), but it’s statistically significant. I will write this until I’m blue in the face: education is positively related to religious attendance in the United States.

But if you break it down by decade of birth, the patterns that emerge are a bit more nuanced. For instance, among people born in the 1940s (or earlier), it’s reversed from the overall sample. Among very old Americans, the ones who are least likely to go to church are those who stopped at high school. (For what it’s worth, I think a lot of what we “know” about social science was true for older cohorts, and we just never updated our priors.)

Look at those born in the 1950s and 1960s—there’s a positive relationship between education and attendance, and the effect is especially large for the latter group (about nine percentage points). For younger cohorts, the impact of education on increased in-person attendance is more muted, but it’s still positive in most cases. Let me state this clearly: there is no birth cohort (aside from those born before 1950) in this data where the statement, “Educated people are less religiously active,” is empirically true. Sometimes there’s no impact, but it certainly is never in the negative direction.

However, that conclusion is completely reversed when we look at the share who say that they “participated in religious services online or on TV.”

In the full sample, the level of education associated with the highest participation in virtual worship is among folks who only possess a high school diploma. About 20% of them are watching online every week, compared to only 12% of people who have earned a graduate degree. In other words, the impact of education on online religious attendance is the exact opposite of how it works for in-person attendance.

This pattern holds across nearly every single birth cohort, too (save for the very youngest adults). In many cohorts, the share of folks with graduate degrees who are attending weekly online is six to ten points lower than the share among those who stopped after high school. There’s just no other way to say this: educated folks simply do not watch online that much at all.

Take, for instance, people born in the 1980s who have graduate degrees. When asked about in-person attendance, 23% indicated that they were there every week. On the question of watching online, it was only 10%. That’s not a small difference.

But let me show you how these two modalities of worship overlap with each other by pulling together a heatmap of attendance patterns by level of education.

The biggest cell for each heatmap is in the bottom left—that’s the never/never category. This makes up 20% of those who stopped at high school and 23% of the sample who earned at least a bachelor’s degree. That’s something to keep in mind here: over 20% of Americans have no relationship to church at all.

But scan the top row—among the HS-or-less crowd, 20% of folks are attending weekly online. For those with a college degree, weekly online attendance is about half that rate (11%). That’s what we saw in the prior analysis: online attendance is much more widespread among folks at the bottom end of the educational spectrum.

That’s the huge upshot I see from this data at this point: education does not meaningfully distinguish Americans in terms of weekly in-person religious attendance, but it sharply differentiates who participates online. Americans with less formal education are far more likely to engage in religious services online, either exclusively or alongside in-person attendance.

Let me simplify this into a single graph that should get my point across more cleanly:

The two bars on the left represent weekly in-person church attendance rates. What do you notice? Well, those who have finished at least four years of college are about 1.4 percentage points more likely to be going to a house of worship on a regular basis compared to those who stopped at high school. That difference is small, but it is statistically significant. Educated folks are slightly more likely to show up on Sunday.

But when it comes to watching online, the gap is an absolute chasm. About 20% of those in the low-education category are watching online every week. It’s just 11.6% of those who have earned a bachelor’s degree. As I mentioned earlier, online attendance is much more prominent among those who have earned no more than a high school diploma.

Here’s a fun stat—I calculated the share of each education category that attended either in person or online weekly or more. Among those with a high school diploma, it was 34%. Among those with a bachelor’s degree, it was 30%. You can easily guess why: a whole bunch of folks in the former category are watching online.

But there are mediating factors that need to be considered. What about the possibility that income can actually erase the education gap? Said another way: if a person earned no more than a high school diploma but makes $100,000 per year, does that make up for the lower level of education?

I love this graph because there are so many layers to it. Let’s look at the in-person attendance result first. Who is the most likely to show up on Sunday? It’s college-educated folks who make $50K–$100K per year. To infer a bit from this: it’s white-collar, middle-class professionals who are the most religiously active in the United States. When income rises above $100K, attendance drops a whopping 7.5 percentage points.

Think about this finding, too: among people with a high school diploma who make less than $50K per year, 23.2% indicate weekly in-person attendance. That’s the exact same rate as folks with a college degree making more than $100K per year. So the impact of income on attendance is actually curvilinear—low on the ends and high in the middle of the spectrum.

That’s not at all true in the bottom graph, which shows online religious attendance. Generally speaking, those with higher levels of income are less likely to be watching services virtually. The effect among those with high school diplomas is more muted, though. The total decline in virtual attendance is about five percentage points when moving from the bottom income bracket to the top one.

For those with a college degree, the drop is much more dramatic. In fact, it’s a decline of more than 50%. Among those with a bachelor’s degree who make less than $50K per year, weekly online attendance is 17.7%. It’s only 8.4% among those making six figures as a family.

Here’s an odd little tidbit of trivia: no one is more likely to go to a house of worship this weekend than a college-educated person making a middle-of-the-road income. And no one is less likely to watch online than someone with a bachelor’s degree who makes at least $100K per year. Data is fun, right?

Now, from an earlier bit of analysis, I know that there were some pretty big gaps when race enters the picture. I wanted to check if that persisted when dividing the sample by educational attainment.

Note that among White Americans who have earned a college degree, only 8% are watching weekly online. That’s about half the rate of White Americans who stopped at a high school diploma. Meanwhile, White college-educated folks are just slightly more likely to attend in person compared to their less-educated counterparts.

For African Americans in this sample, the baseline level of attendance is simply much higher across the board compared to any other racial group. In terms of in-person attendance, 36% of those with a college degree are showing up every week—that’s ten points higher than those who stopped at high school. When it comes to online attendance, there’s essentially no education gap among Black respondents.

The Hispanic numbers are a bit of a head-scratcher compared to the previous two racial groups. Among Hispanics, those with a college degree are actually five points less likely to go in person compared to those with a high school diploma or less. That’s the only instance where educated folks are less religiously active when it comes to in-person attendance. But look at online worship: 24% of those in the low-education group attend weekly online; it’s about half that rate among degree holders (12%).

But let me try to pull all these disparate pieces together into a single bit of analysis to cap this off. I specified a regression model with weekly attendance (in each modality) as the dependent variable. Then I controlled for age, race, and gender and analyzed how income and education work together to shape religious activity.

The graph on the left is in-person attendance. You’ll notice that for the bottom two income buckets (those making less than $100,000 per year), people with a college degree are more likely to be in church each week. But as I mentioned before, attendance this Sunday is most common among those making middle-class incomes. Once you get to the top income bracket, education doesn’t matter at all. For either group, about a quarter are going in person.

For online attendance, the pattern is much different. Among folks who make less than $50K per year, those with a lower level of education are just slightly more likely to watch services online every week. But when moving to the middle income level, the education gap completely disappears. However, it opens back up again among those who make six figures a year. For this subset of Americans, a college degree matters a lot. Among folks who have earned a bachelor’s degree, less than 10% watch online. For those who stopped at high school, online attendance is nearly double that rate (about 17%).

For the pastors and ministry leaders who have followed along thus far, I know what you’re probably thinking: this seems more about social science than the practice of ministry. But let me offer two possible explanations for what this might mean for a local house of worship.

  1. A simple explanation is that folks who don’t have a whole lot of education wouldn’t have shown up in person before the streaming explosion. Now they have an option to watch online. In that sense, this is actually filling a void in their spiritual lives. That’s the most positive spin on this data.

  2. The other way to look at this is much more bleak. These low-education, low-income folks who are watching online would have been going in person, but it’s simply easier to pull up YouTube and watch the stream. In some ways, it may be making them more socially isolated—which, by any measure, is a bad thing.

One simple maxim that I use all the time is this: “Dropping out begets dropping out.” You can see it across the data: folks who didn’t further their education are more likely to be less religious, attend church less regularly, rarely engage in the political process, and be more socially isolated.

Is online church making that whole downward spiral of isolation even worse? It’s impossible to say for certain, but it’s something that needs to be considered very carefully—not just for the spiritual lives of those watching your stream, but for their social lives as well.

Ryan P. Burge is a professor of practice at the Danforth Center on Religion and Politics at Washington University.
