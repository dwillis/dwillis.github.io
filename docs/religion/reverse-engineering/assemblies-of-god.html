The Assemblies of God in 2024

A big denomination with some really healthy statistical trends

People often ask me what trends I see in churches that are growing. I think there are a few big-picture factors that really matter. One is charismatic worship. That’s become the norm for a lot of fast-growing evangelical churches—not just in the United States, but around the world. Another common thread among growing parts of the faith landscape is that these churches tend to be non-denominational and anything but hierarchical. In other words, being tied to one of the major Protestant families often seems to slow growth rather than help it.

But there’s one major denomination that manages to buck that trend: the Assemblies of God. They have one big advantage—charismatic worship—but they’re also still a coherent denomination. Despite being fairly “institutional,” they’ve managed to post nearly continuous growth over the last five decades. That’s something almost no other major denomination in the United States can say.

Whenever I put together denominational reports, the story is usually one of decline. The metrics almost always point downward, and the future doesn’t look any brighter than the past for most of the old stalwarts of American Protestantism. But that’s not the case for the Assemblies of God. In fact, their best days might still be ahead.

Let’s start by tracking total membership in the AG since 1975.

As you can see, the line goes up for the Assemblies of God, and it does so in a remarkably steady way. There aren’t any bursts of exponential growth; instead, it’s a slow, consistent climb of about 2–3 percent per year. That’s actually a really healthy sign. This pattern continued almost completely uninterrupted from 1975 through 2018. Then, things started to shift a bit.

There’s no doubt that COVID-19 disrupted the Assemblies of God’s long-term trajectory. That will be a theme throughout the rest of this piece. Total membership in AG churches peaked right before the pandemic in 2019 at about 3.3 million members. That’s an increase of roughly two million since 1975. Not bad at all.

Now let’s zoom in on that most recent stretch, from 2014 through 2024, to get a more detailed look at what the last decade has looked like.

You can see that really steady rise between 2014 and 2019, which added about 150,000 people during that period. Like I said above, that’s solid growth. Not spectacular, but definitely healthy. Then the pandemic shut the world down, and that shows up clearly in the numbers. In just a single year—2020—the Assemblies of God lost about 330,000 members. The gains from the previous five years were wiped out twice over in one fell swoop.

But that was clearly the low point. The losses didn’t continue into 2022, and membership began to climb again in 2023 and 2024. The current total sits at 3,059,461. That’s just a bit higher than the 3.04 million members the AG reported back in 2011. So things are back on the upswing, but the recovery isn’t yet complete.

What about attendance?

You can basically see the same general pattern here: a steady, healthy rise in the number of worshippers from the mid-1970s through the mid-2010s. In fact, attendance nearly doubled over those forty years. But the pandemic took a real toll on those numbers. The peak was just over two million worshippers sometime between 2016 and 2018, before dropping by about 300,000 in 2020 during the height of COVID restrictions. That 1.7 million figure is roughly where the Assemblies of God stood around the year 2000.

Let’s zoom in again on the last ten years of data, because there’s something important to notice there.

There’s that steady rise again between 2014 and 2018—about 100,000 more attenders across that five-year stretch. But then take a look at 2019. Attendance dips just below two million. It’s only about 40,000 fewer people, but it drops the total back to roughly 2016 levels. I’m not entirely sure what to make of that. It could just be a one-year blip—you can see a few of those in the line graph above. Or it might have been the first hint that things were starting to turn downward before COVID-19 was even on the national radar.

Either way, the post-COVID numbers show a solid rebound. Attendance rose by about 50,000 in 2021, doubled that increase between 2022 and 2023, and then jumped another 120,000 in 2024. Not bad at all. But it’s worth noting that attendance in the Assemblies of God in 2024 is basically the same as it was in 2015—a decade lost, essentially.

So this sets up a pretty interesting little data exercise. I wanted to see what attendance might have looked like if COVID-19 had never happened. The modeling is straightforward: I took the data from 2009 through 2019, fit a trend line through those figures, and then extended that line out through 2024. In other words, assuming the pre-pandemic trend had continued uninterrupted, what would those next few years have looked like without the lockdowns?

You can see that the trend line points upward, assuming attendance would continue to grow by about 20,000 people per year. That’s obviously not what happened. In fact, the opposite occurred. Instead of a 20,000-person gain in 2020 to reach about 2.05 million, the actual figure dropped to 1.75 million. After that sharp decline, attendance began to climb again from 2021 through 2024, gradually moving closer to that projected trend line.

Let’s take a look at the actual figures from 2020 onward to get a clearer picture of how things have looked in the post-pandemic period.

In 2020, the actual number of attendees lagged behind the projection by about 300,000 people—nearly 15 percent below the trend line. Things got even worse in 2021, when attendance fell almost 400,000 short of expectations, a 19.2 percent gap. But the good news is that the last couple of years have shown clear improvement, which has to be encouraging for Assemblies of God leadership.

By 2024, the gap between projected and actual attendance had narrowed to about 173,000 people, or 8.1 percent. The key thing to notice is that this percentage keeps dropping. That’s a strong signal of steady recovery. I ran the numbers, and here’s an interesting takeaway: if the current trajectory holds, the AG’s attendance will have completely erased the COVID-era decline by around 2029 or 2030. In other words, the bars in that previous chart will finally rise above the trend line. Pretty impressive.

Now let’s turn to inflows for the Assemblies of God. They track three main categories: water baptisms, Spirit baptisms, and the always-mysterious “other” category. Here’s how those trends look going all the way back to 1979.

I think of this graph in three phases. The first runs from 1979 through the mid-1990s. During this period, conversions into AG churches were growing, but still relatively modest—around 350,000 per year in the early 1990s. Then there’s a noticeable jump in the “other” category. Between 1990 and 1995, that figure hovered between 130,000 and 150,000, but in 1996 it suddenly spiked to about 300,000 and then stayed above 250,000 for roughly the next decade. My best guess is that there was some kind of policy change at play, though I can’t say for sure.

The final phase is what’s happened since 2020. There was an obvious nosedive in baptism numbers during the lockdowns, which is completely understandable. The 2020 figures looked a lot like what we saw back in 1994. But since then, there’s been a clear rebound. In 2024, total inflows reached about 530,000 people—a number only exceeded twice in the entire time series (1997 and 1999). Said simply, 2024 was a banner year for new people joining an Assemblies of God church.

Here’s a closer look at what’s been happening with AG conversions since 2020.

Spirit baptisms have always made up a relatively small share of total conversions. There were about 62,000 in 2020, rising to 84,000 in the most recent data. The growth in water baptisms, though, is much more dramatic—jumping from 94,000 to 169,000, an increase of 75,000. That means AG churches across the United States are conducting roughly 3,250 water baptisms every weekend. Solid growth, no question about it.

The “other” category is still the largest contributor. In 2024, about 277,000 new additions to the AG came through this group. My best guess is that this reflects people transferring their membership from another denomination into the Assemblies of God. But if you know more about how these “other” cases are categorized, feel free to chime in down in the comments.

One last metric before we wrap up—and it might be the most important one in this entire discussion: racial composition.

In 2001, 71 percent of Assemblies of God members were White, 16 percent were Hispanic, and 6 percent were Black, with all other racial groups making up the remaining 7 percent. But that balance shifted fairly quickly. By 2007, the White share had dropped by eight points, while Hispanic and Black members each gained about three points. That trend continued throughout the rest of the dataset.

In the most recent data, only 55 percent of the Assemblies of God are White, while nearly a quarter (23 percent) are Hispanic. About 11 percent are African American, and another 11 percent come from all other racial backgrounds combined. For comparison, according to the Census Bureau, about 58 percent of Americans are White (non-Hispanic), 12 percent are Black, and 19 percent are Hispanic. This may be the most insightful statistic in this entire analysis: the Assemblies of God today looks almost exactly like the broader U.S. population.

All in all, this is a remarkably strong report for the Assemblies of God. Growth has continued across nearly every key metric—membership, attendance, conversions, and more. It’s actually hard to find any data here that points in a negative direction. And honestly, it feels strange to write this (because I almost never get to), but things are genuinely healthy in this one large denomination in the United States.
