Are College Teams Passing More on First Down?

2022-11-10 
library(tidyverse)
library(cfbfastR)

tictoc::tic()
pbp <- data.frame()
seasons <- 2014:cfbfastR:::most_recent_cfb_season()
progressr::with_progress({

  pbp <- cfbfastR::load_cfb_pbp(seasons)
})
tictoc::toc()
## 43.313 sec elapsed

Most college football teams run more often on first down than they pass. Unless your coach is Mike Leach or you play for Indiana this year, that is.

Leach, the current coach at Mississippi State and inventor of the prolific “Air Raid” offense, has long favored passing over the ground game, but most teams don’t play that way. In the Big Ten, the number of first-down passing plays has hovered around four in 10 until the recently. This season it’s currently at one in three. The Hoosiers, who started out the season with three straight wins before losing their last seven games, are the exception: they pass about half the time on first down.

The Big Ten has long had a reputation as a run-first conference, so I wanted to see if that had changed at all when it comes to play choice on first downs. Using play-by-play data from cfbfastR, I isolated all first down plays by season and team since 2014, and then did the same for all first-down pass plays. I’m specifically including plays with penalties, since the intent of the play-call is what I’m interested in, not whether the play succeeded. To start with, I’ll look at all teams, not just the Big Ten.

first_down_plays <- pbp %>% 
  filter(down == 1) %>% 
  group_by(season, pos_team) %>% 
  summarise(all_plays = n())
first_down_pass_plays <- pbp %>%
  filter(pass == 1, down == 1) %>%
  group_by(season, pos_team) %>%
  summarise(pass_plays = n())

Let’s take those two dataframes of all first-down plays and first-down pass plays, calculate a percentage and see what we have.

combined_plays <- first_down_plays %>% 
  inner_join(first_down_pass_plays, by=c("season", "pos_team")) %>% 
  mutate(pass_pct = pass_plays/all_plays*100)

Leach-coached teams represent four of the top 10 with the highest first-down passing percentages. Just looking at this dataframe there are some outliers to deal with: in particular, there are some teams with very low pass percentages and some with 100%. Both are possible, but more important is removing teams with a very small number of total first-down plays. Could Maine really have run 57 first-down plays in the 2019 season? Let’s try limiting our data to teams that have run at least 100 total plays (which seems safely conservative).

combined_plays <- combined_plays %>% filter(all_plays >= 100)

Now I’ll make a line chart of these results, grouping by team:

ggplot() + 
  geom_line(data=combined_plays, aes(x=season, y=pass_pct, group=pos_team))

Well. If there’s any kind of pattern here beyond the traditional hairball, it might be that the first-down pass play percentage has gone down slightly over time. Let’s see if I can confirm that at all:

first_down_plays_by_season <- pbp %>% 
  filter(down == 1) %>% 
  group_by(season) %>% 
  summarise(all_plays = n())

first_down_pass_plays_by_season <- pbp %>%
  filter(pass == 1, down == 1) %>%
  group_by(season) %>%
  summarise(pass_plays = n())

combined_plays_by_season <- first_down_plays_by_season %>% 
  inner_join(first_down_pass_plays_by_season, by="season") %>% 
  mutate(pass_pct = pass_plays/all_plays*100)

View(combined_plays_by_season)

I’m not super excited about the number of first-down plays that have no season value, but for those plays that have a season, the trend is somewhat downwards in the past two seasons, although it’s possible that’s because we have more data for more recent seasons. I’m not confident enough in this data to say that what I’m seeing is an actual decline.

Next, I’ll move to looking at Big Ten teams.

first_down_plays_big_ten <- pbp %>% 
  filter(down == 1, offense_conference == 'Big Ten') %>% 
  group_by(season, pos_team) %>% 
  summarise(all_plays = n())

first_down_pass_plays_big_ten <- pbp %>%
  filter(pass == 1, down == 1, offense_conference == 'Big Ten') %>%
  group_by(season, pos_team) %>%
  summarise(pass_plays = n())

combined_plays_big_ten <- first_down_plays_big_ten %>% 
  inner_join(first_down_pass_plays_big_ten, by=c("season", "pos_team")) %>% 
  mutate(pass_pct = pass_plays/all_plays*100)

I’d like to get a sense of each team’s first-down play-calling tendencies, so I’ll use facet_wrap to generate individual charts.

ggplot() + 
  geom_line(data=combined_plays_big_ten, aes(x=season, y=pass_pct, group=pos_team)) +
  facet_wrap(~pos_team)
## Warning: Removed 14 row(s) containing missing values (geom_path).

Indiana’s jump this season is immediately evidence, as are declines for Illinois, Purdue, Rutgers and Penn State. Let’s add an average from the Big Ten schools, using just the plays we have seasons for:

all_first_down_plays_big_ten <- pbp %>% 
  filter(down == 1, offense_conference == 'Big Ten', !is.na(season)) %>% 
  summarise(all_plays = n())

all_first_down_pass_plays_big_ten <- pbp %>%
  filter(pass == 1, down == 1, offense_conference == 'Big Ten', !is.na(season)) %>%
  summarise(pass_plays = n())

average <- all_first_down_pass_plays_big_ten$pass_plays/all_first_down_plays_big_ten$all_plays * 100

ggplot() + 
  geom_hline(yintercept=average, color="blue") + 
  geom_line(data=combined_plays_big_ten, aes(x=season, y=pass_pct, group=pos_team)) +
  facet_wrap(~pos_team)
## Warning: Removed 14 row(s) containing missing values (geom_path).

Now we can see the consistent run-first schools: Wisconsin, Minnesota, Iowa and Rutgers. We also can see that Maryland, after dramatically increasing its first-down pass plays between 2018 and 2020, has come back closer to the conference average.

But Indiana stands alone this season. What about historically?

Removing teams with fewer than 100 total first-down plays makes Leach-coached offenses even more prominent on this list - six of the top 10 are his - and Illinois’ 2015 team now gets in the first ten as well. By historical standards, IU is still pretty far from the mark. By Big Ten standards, they are fun-and-gunning it this season.
